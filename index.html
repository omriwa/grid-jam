How Anti-Drone GNSS Jamming
Stops GPS-Guided Weapons

A plain-language explanation of why conventional jammers fail against CRPA-equipped military drones, and how Grid-Jam's distributed array solves the problem.

  • 1
    GPS Is the Weapon's Brain
    Modern drones and missiles use all four GNSS constellations (GPS, GLONASS, Galileo, BeiDou) across six frequency bands (1.1–1.6 GHz) to navigate with meter-level precision. Disrupting GNSS navigation forces the weapon into "dead reckoning" — it misses its target by tens to hundreds of meters.
  • 2
    Why Single Jammers Fail: CRPA Defeats Them
    Military anti-drone countermeasures are undermined by CRPA — Controlled Reception Pattern Antennas. A 4-element CRPA automatically nullifies jamming signals from up to 3 directions simultaneously. This makes every conventional single-unit or triple-jammer anti-drone system completely ineffective against the Shahed-136 and equivalent platforms.
  • 3
    Grid-Jam's Solution: Overwhelm CRPA from All Directions
    EPI places GNSS jammer units in a geometric array surrounding the protected site. The distributed grid attacks from more simultaneous directions than the CRPA can nullify — overwhelming the anti-jam filter and breaking GPS lock entirely, across all six frequency bands.
  • 4
    Last 5 km Anti-Drone Zone
    EPI's omnidirectional jammer grid — including defeat of 4+ element CRPA — provides continuous GPS denial for at least the last 5 km of drone or cruise missile approach. The weapon flies its final leg without accurate navigation, missing its target by statistically unpredictable distances.
  • 5
    50 km Ballistic & Hypersonic Missile Defense Corridor
    Grid-Jam's grouped array of vertical high-power directional jammers covers the full 50 km atmospheric re-entry phase of ballistic and hypersonic missiles — the only commercially available GNSS jamming solution with documented ballistic missile coverage.
  • 6
    Integrated Counter-UAS Air Defense Layer
    EPI integrates with national air-defense warning systems for automatic activation, or is manually controlled locally or country-wide. Operates standalone or alongside existing radar and interceptor systems — adding a non-kinetic defense layer with zero per-engagement cost.
